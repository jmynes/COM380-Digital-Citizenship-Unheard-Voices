Enacting Citizenship 4/22 Interview - Anonymous

Introduction to Participatory Politics

My interview subject, unidentified by request, is an actively engaged citizen and activist in Chicago. I conducted our interview over a Google Hangouts call, and have selected some highlights from our discusion for listening below. For the ease and natural flow of language, I will refer to him as Wallace.

Wallace became interested in particpatory politics in late high school, volunteering for a labor union. He was largely focused around the academic aspects, focusing heavily on policy and debating classmates with the intention of winning over his peers rhetorically.

I did a Senior year research presentation on immigration, and the types of abuses that can go on with employers taking people's passports, basically turning them into slaves or indentured servants.

Interview subject, 4/22/2018

Collectives & Engagement

A common thread in our discussion was shedding the weight of being a "policy wonk" full-time, becoming radicalized, and opting instead for direct involvement and activism.

Wallace identified some of his introductory political action early into our conversation, detailing his work with Environmental Justice, a People and Planet First budget rally. The primary demand of this rally was to provide budget for good-paying green jobs, and participation motivated Wallace greatly.

Attending that rally made me feel so empowered... made me feel like there are way more powerful things that I could do as part of a collective

Interview subject, 4/22/2018

Citizenship vs. citizenship

In our increasingly globalized society, the concept of 'Citizenship' is one of interest. Instead of Citizenship as status, citizenship as practice manifests in the habitus of a nations inhabitants. Over time, through activism, spread of culture, resistance, and exercise of rights, an individual cultivates citizenship in a way that most nations do not formally recognize.

Small 'c' citizenship—defined more by acts than by status—has many forms. Isin and Nielsen assert, "Theorizing acts is part of a larger body of work that proposes a way of investigating genealogies of citizenship as a generalized question of other-ness that includes strangers but also outsiders and aliens." One way of enacting citizenship is through activism, which Isin and Nielsen describe as "writing the script" that defines citizenship. Though again their status may be overlooked or suppressed by law, through acts of citizenship laws may be shaped or overturned; and some bending of the law may often occur in the process.

Acts of Citizenship (2008), pg. 15-20, 37-39 Engin F. Isin, Greg M. Nielsen

Wallace sees eye-to-eye with this analysis. When asked if he could conceptualize the difference between these types of citizenship, he immediately expressed familiarity with the subject. This is a topic that concerns Wallace dearly, and drives him to stand in solidarity with DREAMers and fight for the advancement of immigrant rights.

[...] the whole preoccupation with papers, documentation... is ridiculous; I'd like to see a borderless world without nationalities or Citizenship at all.

Interview subject, 4/22/2018
